# Hyponatremia: Quick ICU Reference

## 1. Acute/Severe Symptomatic Management (Emergencies)
* **Symptoms:** Seizures, coma, severe confusion, respiratory arrest.
* **Action:** Give 3% Hypertonic Saline immediately. 
* **Dosing:** 100-150 mL IV bolus over 10-20 minutes. Can repeat x2 if severe symptoms persist.
* **Goal:** Increase serum Na by 4-6 mEq/L to arrest symptoms. *Do not exceed this acutely.*

## 2. Diagnostic Algorithm (The 3 Steps)
**Step 1: Check Serum Osmolality (Is it true hyponatremia?)**
* **Normal (275-295):** Pseudohyponatremia (hyperlipidemia, hyperproteinemia).
* **High (>295):** Hypertonic hyponatremia (hyperglycemia, mannitol).
    * *Correction:* Add 1.6 mEq/L to Na for every 100 mg/dL glucose > 100.
* **Low (<275):** True hypotonic hyponatremia $\rightarrow$ Proceed to Step 2.

**Step 2: Check Urine Osmolality (Is ADH active?)**
* **< 100 mOsm/kg:** Primary polydipsia, beer potomania, tea & toast syndrome. ADH is appropriately suppressed.
* **> 100 mOsm/kg:** ADH is active. $\rightarrow$ Proceed to Step 3.

**Step 3: Assess Volume Status & Check Urine Sodium**
* **Hypovolemic (Dry):**
    * Urine Na < 20: Extrarenal loss (GI losses, sweating, third-spacing).
    * Urine Na > 20: Renal loss (Diuretics, mineralocorticoid deficiency, cerebral salt wasting).
* **Euvolemic (Clinically normal):**
    * Urine Na > 20, Uosm > 100: SIADH, hypothyroidism, secondary adrenal insufficiency.
* **Hypervolemic (Edematous):**
    * Urine Na < 20: Heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome (low effective arterial blood volume).
    * Urine Na > 20: Acute or chronic renal failure.

## 3. Safe Correction Limits
* **Rule of 6s:** "Six a day makes sense for safety."
* **Maximum Limits:** * Goal: 4-6 mEq/L/day.
    * Absolute max: 8 mEq/L in 24 hours.
* **High Risk for ODS:** Malnutrition, alcoholism, severe liver disease, baseline Na < 105. Strict max of 4-6 mEq/L/24h.
